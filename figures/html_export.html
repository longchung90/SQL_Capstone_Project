Slide set 4
Insights discovered — going deeper & broader
Hypothesis outcomes, new metrics created, and relationships found in the data.
Hypothesis outcomes
H1Party predicts topic — Confirmed ✅
House & Senate members of the same party communicate more similarly than cross-party colleagues in the same chamber. Cosine divergence grew 0.31→0.61 (2009–2017) — the vocabulary gap widens every year.
↪ New hypothesis added: polarization is accelerating, not stabilizing.
H2Influence concentrated — Strongly confirmed ✅
Top 10% hold 62.5% of all retweets. Top 20% hold 79.3%. Mean/median ratio 47.5× confirms power-law. Senate achieves 1.8× median reach vs House.
↪ Hypothesis was conservative — reality is more extreme.
H3Election years drive spikes — Revised ⚠️
2013 (non-election) produced +165.7% YoY — largest spike in dataset. Average election-year growth: only +42.7%.
↪ Reframed: high-stakes legislative moments drive surges. Elections are just one type.
New metrics created
Metric 1 — Lobbying Leverage Score (LLS)
Why: Mean retweets are dominated by viral outliers. Lobbyists need stable, consistent influence ranking.

Formula: median_rt × log(tweet_count+1) × chamber_mult (1.8 Senate, 1.0 House)

Value: Scores any member including newly elected — independent of historical totals.
Tier 1: LLS > 20 Tier 2: 10–20 Monitor: <10
Metric 2 — Bipartisan Window Score (BWS)
Why: A state can be "contested" but have one party dormant. BWS captures when both parties are simultaneously active and balanced.

Formula: competition_score × min(D,R) / max(D,R)

Key rule: 500 D tweets + 5 R tweets → BWS ≈ 0. Balance required, not just presence.
IL, CO, IN → top states Peak: Sep–Oct
Key relationships discovered
📐 Correlation 1: Senate × Retweets (r = +0.31, p < 0.001)
Senators outperform House members by 1.8× on median retweets after controlling for volume. Media amplification — not just audience size — creates this structural premium.
😡 Correlation 2: Negative sentiment × Retweets (r = −0.19, p < 0.001)
More negative tweets earn more retweets. Effect stronger for Democrats (r = −0.23) vs Republicans (r = −0.14). Framing a policy failure outperforms framing a policy solution.
🗺️ Geographic: Party competition ≠ simultaneous activity
A state with competition score 0.98 can still score BWS ≈ 0 if one party is inactive that month. Nevada dropped in BWS; Illinois, Colorado, Indiana emerged as true bipartisan windows.
Slide set 5
Recommendations & actions
Four decisions Lobbyists4America can make on Monday, each grounded in data.
1
Restructure outreach by party, not chamber
Why: Party explains vocabulary 2× better than chamber. Chamber-based lists use the wrong organizing principle.
Action: Merge Senate + House contact lists into separate D and R playbooks. Two messaging strategies, not four.
2
Lead every bipartisan campaign with "health care"
Why: The #1 bigram for both parties simultaneously. No other topic offers equivalent bipartisan reach in 1.24M tweets.
Action: Use "health care" as entry frame before branching to party-specific language. Veterans and jobs are safe secondary frames.
3
Weight Senate targets 2–3× vs House targets
Why: 1.8× structural reach advantage confirmed by correlation (r=+0.31) and regression (+0.61 standardized coefficient).
Action: Senate target = 2× base budget. Tier 1 Senate members (LLS > 1,000): apply 3× weight.
4
Run IL / CO / IN in Sep–Oct; avoid August
Why: Highest BWS states with peak bipartisan windows in Q3–Q4. August is congressional recess — lowest activity floor in dataset.
Action: Schedule cross-party campaigns: Illinois Sep, Colorado Oct, Indiana Sep. Zero campaigns in August.
⚠️ Honest caveats & next steps
Dataset limitations
Covers 2008–2017. Twitter's 280-char limit, follower growth, and political intensity all postdate the archive. Patterns likely persisted — but member scores need re-validation against current data.
Recommended next steps
• Pull 2018–2024 Twitter/X data; rerun LLS + BWS
• Add follower-count normalization to regression
• Build real-time LLS dashboard in Tableau / DataBricks
• A/B test bipartisan framing in live campaigns

-- Enrich tweets with political metadata
SELECT
    t.tweet_id, t.user_id, t.text, t.created_at,
    t.retweet_count, t.favorite_count,
    l.party, l.chamber, l.state,
    YEAR(t.created_at)  AS year,
    MONTH(t.created_at) AS month
FROM tweets t
LEFT JOIN legislators l
    ON LOWER(t.screen_name) = LOWER(l.twitter)
WHERE l.party IN ('Republican','Democrat')
ORDER BY t.created_at;
-- Result: 924,517 matched rows (74.8% of 1,243,370)
-- 138 members retained but excluded from party comparisons
-- Lobbying Leverage Score per member
SELECT
    m.name, m.party, m.chamber, m.state,
    MEDIAN(t.retweet_count)  AS median_rt,
    COUNT(t.tweet_id)        AS tweet_count,
    ROUND(
      MEDIAN(t.retweet_count)
      * LN(COUNT(t.tweet_id) + 1)
      * (CASE WHEN m.chamber='Senate' THEN 1.8 ELSE 1.0 END),
    2) AS LLS
FROM tweets t
JOIN legislators m ON t.user_id = m.user_id
WHERE m.party IN ('Republican','Democrat')
GROUP BY m.name, m.party, m.chamber, m.state
HAVING tweet_count >= 10
ORDER BY LLS DESC LIMIT 20;
-- Tiers: LLS > 20 = Priority 1 | 10-20 = Tier 2 | <10 = Monitor
